Ally

Ally is an expert app for job seekers searching for quick industry specific insights and actionable advice when applying for the job of their dreams.

This was a project that I developed during my User Experience Design course at Career Foundry. The work started in January 2020. The deliverables were ready for handoff in May same year.

My approach

During the course of the project I was following user-centered design framework that allowed me to organize and plan my tasks without losing focus on the problem and the user.

Research

An important step in the process was to get familiarized with competitors on the market and understand their approach to online advisory. I’ve looked at two applications: Quikconsult and JustAnswer.

Next, it was time to interview potential users and gather their needs and information about their behaviors. I recruited people who fitted the user profile, that is were actively looking for job opportunities or looked for jobs in the past three months.

Top actionable insights from the interviews:

  • people were most interested in consulting with industry practitioners or recruiters, rather than career coaches
  • people need both written way of communication such as messages or chat and verbal communication phone calls or video conferences
  • people want quick and easy access to consultation, so well functioning and intuitive search functionality is crucial
  • people most value years of experience, expertise and price rate as choice factors

Low- to mid-fidelity wireframes

After grinding through ideas it was time to put pen to paper and start working on sketches and low-fidelity prototype to collect rough drafts of content layouts.

Conceptual low-fidelity wireframes served as a basis for creating mid-fidelity wireframes with Adobe XD. Later, using the wireframes, I drew out the interactions and supplemented them with annotations that served as a static prototype.

After modifying some details and increasing the fidelity of my wireframes, I started setting up interactions for a clickable prototype to use it for testing. At the end of this phase, there were three features ready for testing: send a message to an expert, set up a payment method and book a call with an expert. Review the prototype here

Usability testing

After setting up the interactive prototype in the previous phase, I have started planning for usability testing sessions to check the learnability of the main features of the app. I started with recruiting participants, who, similarly as for user interviews’, were looking for a new job at the time or have been in the process in the past few months.

After I ran the test with all participants, I analyzed the notes and recordings from the sessions using affinity mapping to discover patterns and themes.

Most urgent issues for immediate redesign:

  • chat messages not available from expert profile page,
  • payments are difficult to spot in the account menu,
  • there is no possibility to select time zone in the call booking flow,
  • call fee not pronounced enough, users were surprised or frustrated,
  • users expected to be able to add a new call without having to visit expert profile.

Visual design

Having fixed the top usability issues and some other minor problems, I have set off to refine the UI of the design. The works included:

  • applying 12-column grid layout that’s comfortable for responsible design work,
  • reviewing the design with consideration to Gestalt laws of grouping and the principles of visual design,
  • preference testing to support design choices,
  • preparing a style guide,
  • and, finally, a polished prototype for further rounds of reviews.

Final prototype

Having iterated on the design after the peers reviews and accessibility corrections, I set up the final prototype for this training project. There were three features developed:

  • send a message to an expert
  • set up a payment method
  • book a call with an expert

Retrospective

Preparing this case study was a perfect opportunity to take a moment and evaluate the successes of the project, as well as identify the areas for improvement.

I was positively surprised with how easy it was to conduct the usability testing thanks to well prepared script and tasks. I also spent a lot of time studying the script, so that I don’t have to focus on it as much during the sessions. That allowed me to better concentrate on the participants and follow how they interacted with the app.

Multiple rounds of iterations on the prototypes, provoked by tutor/mentor feedback, testing or peer review, greatly helped with evolution of the product. Receiving critical feedback turned out to be very motivational for me and helped my creative process.

On the other hand, I see some potential for improvement with the way I conducted initial user interviews. At times, I focused on noting down what the participants were saying to such extent that I missed the meaning of the comments and failed to follow up on important remarks.

In the future, to not have to worry about missing any important point, I’d use recording software on top of taking notes. That will allow me to ask follow-up questions, and gain more insights for the ideation phase.
